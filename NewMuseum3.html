Seqenenre Tao II

Seqenenre Tao II is one of the greatest kings of Egypt, as he was the first to begin the actual fighting to expel the Hyksos from the land, a task that was completed by his son Ahmose I. He was the son of King Senakhtenre Tao I (also called Seqenenre Tao I) and Queen Tetisheri. The dates of his reign are uncertain, but it is believed that he ascended to the throne in 1560 BCE or 1558 BCE.

The beginning of his war with the Hyksos
King Seqenenre Tao II was the first king to start fighting the Hyksos. There is a document known as the Sallier Papyrus, which comes in the form of a story attributed to Seqenenre’s era. It tells us how the conflict began between the Hyksos king Apepi (Akenre Apophis) and King Seqenenre. Apepi sent a message from Avaris in the northern Delta to Seqenenre, complaining that the noise of the hippopotamuses in the lake of Thebes was disturbing his sleep—despite the fact that 500 miles separated him from Thebes. He ordered Seqenenre to find any means to silence them. This message was effectively a declaration of war.

His family
Seqenenre Tao II married Queen Ahhotep and had with her: - Kamose, the last king of the Seventeenth Dynasty. - Ahmose I, the first king of the Eighteenth Dynasty.

His death
There are several theories about how Seqenenre died. The most common is that he was killed in battle against the Hyksos. Some theories suggest he was killed while sleeping, as he was lying on his right side when attacked—either because he was asleep or because he had already been wounded and fell onto his right side before receiving the fatal blow. His body was hastily and briefly mummified, likely on the battlefield to prevent decay until it could be transported to Thebes, where a second embalming attempt took place. His mummy was housed in the Egyptian Museum at Tahrir Square in Cairo until it was moved on Saturday, April 3, 2021, to the National Museum of Egyptian Civilization in Fustat. His tomb was discovered in the Dira Abu el-Naga cache in 1881 CE, and the mummy was unwrapped on June 9, 1886, by Gaston Maspero. King Seqenenre died fighting the Hyksos. His skull shows horrific fatal wounds—multiple gashes and fractures from spears and axes. His teeth were clenched tightly against his tongue due to extreme pain in his final moments. His head bears numerous severe injuries: - A dagger stab behind the left ear that reached his neck. - His chest and nose shattered by mace blows. - A war axe cut penetrating the bone above his forehead. Seqenenre was of average height (about 170 cm), had a large head, was muscular, with thick, curly black hair, and was just over 30 years old at the time of his death.

King Kamose

Kamose was the last pharaoh of the Theban Seventeenth Dynasty. He may have been the son of Pharaoh Seqenenre and Queen Ahhotep, and the brother of Ahmose I, founder of the Eighteenth Dynasty. His reign fell at the end of the Second Intermediate Period. Kamose's rule is credited with three years, though scholars prefer to assign him a longer reign of up to five years. His era is highly significant due to the military initiatives he launched against the Hyksos, who had come to rule ancient Egypt. His father, Seqenenre, had begun these efforts but soon lost his life in one of the battles against them. It is believed that his mother (as regent) continued the fighting after Kamose’s death, and his brother Ahmose I completed the campaign, ultimately expelling the Hyksos and unifying Egypt.

His Achievements In the third year of his reign, Kamose sailed north from Thebes along the Nile, fighting small Hyksos groups and destroying their fortifications on his way. As for the major Hyksos cities that confronted him with strong resistance, he besieged them and cut them off from the rest of the Hyksos kingdom until he reached Avaris. In 1954 CE, two stelae were discovered at Karnak, erected to glorify King Kamose’s victories over Apophis, king of the Hyksos. One of these stelae describes Kamose’s victory in Avaris, the Hyksos capital in the Delta, detailing the strategy he followed and his triumphant return to Thebes.

His Death
Kamose died during his war with the Hyksos in 1540 BCE on the outskirts of the city of Imbous, after having entered it victorious and triumphant. He was struck by a treacherous blow from one of the wounded Hyksos, which took his life. His last words were a testament to his brother Ahmose, asking him to inform their great grandmother Tetisheri that he had joined their father and died the same death as Seqenenre. Kamose’s tomb is mentioned in the Abbott Papyrus, which records that his coffin was moved from his original tomb and re-buried in Deir el-Bahri for protection from thieves during the reign of Ramesses IV. It was discovered by Mariette in December 1857 under a pile of earth. Mariette noted that the mummy was in very poor condition. Found with the mummy were: - A gold and silver dagger - A bronze mirror - A braided papyrus on Kamose’s upper arm - A scarab and some amulets The shroud remained in Egypt, while the dagger is now in the Brussels Museum in Belgium, and the mirror is in the Louvre Museum.

King Ahmose

Ahmose I (meaning "born of Yah") was a king of ancient Egypt and the founder of the Eighteenth Dynasty. He was a member of the Theban royal family, the son of King Seqenenre and the brother of the last king of the Seventeenth Dynasty, Kamose. During the reign of his father or grandfather, Thebes rebelled against the Hyksos, rulers of Lower Egypt. When he was seven years old, his father was killed, and around the age of ten, his brother died for unknown reasons after ruling for only three years. Ahmose I ascended the throne after his brother’s death and became known by the name Nebpehtyre ("Lord of the Strength of Re"). *Ahmose* is a theophoric name, a combination of the syllable "Ah" and the form "-moses." The syllable "Ah" refers to Yah. During his reign, he ended the Hyksos invasion, expelled them from the Delta, and restored Thebes’ sovereignty over all of Egypt and its former territories from Nubia to Canaan. He reorganized the country’s administration, reopened quarries and mines, established new trade routes, and launched massive construction projects of a scale not seen since the Middle Kingdom. Ahmose’s reign laid the foundations for the New Kingdom, during which the Egyptian state reached its peak.

His Family
Ahmose descended from the Seventeenth Dynasty of Thebes. His grandfather was Seqenenre Tao, and his grandmother was Tetisheri. They had more than twelve children, including Seqenenre and Ahhotep. Following ancient Egyptian royal traditions, the brother and sister married and had Ahmose I, Kamose, and many daughters. Ahmose followed his father’s customs and married several of his sisters, making his sister Ahmose-Nefertari his primary wife. They had numerous children, including daughters Ahmose-Merytamun and Ahmose-Sitamun, and sons Siamun, Amenhotep I, Ahmose-ankh, and Ramose. They may also have been the parents of Mutnofret, who married Thutmose I. It appears that Ahmose-ankh was Ahmose I’s crown prince, but he died between years 17 and 22 of Ahmose’s reign. Ahmose I was succeeded by his eldest surviving son, Amenhotep I. There was no clear dividing line between the Seventeenth and Eighteenth Dynasties. The later historian Manetho, writing during the Ptolemaic period, considered the final expulsion of the Hyksos—after nearly a century of their presence—and the restoration of Egyptian rule over the entire country a significant enough event to justify the start of a new dynasty.

Military Campaigns
Expulsion of the Hyksos King Seqenenre was the first to attack the Hyksos to fight them and drive them out of Egypt. He was killed in one of his battles with the Hyksos. His son Kamose then continued the war until he cleared Upper Egypt of the Hyksos. Finally, Ahmose expelled the Hyksos from the country entirely. Ahmose led his armies when he was about 19 years old, using advanced weapons such as war chariots. Many people from Thebes joined the army. He and his forces marched to Avaris (modern San el-Hagar), the Hyksos capital, and defeated them there. He then pursued them to Palestine, besieged them in the fortress of Sharuhen, and crushed them until they surrendered. The Hyksos never appeared in history again. This battle took place around 1580 BCE.

Period of his reign
King Ahmose developed the Egyptian army and was the first to introduce war chariots to it (which the Hyksos had used and were the reason for their dominance over Egypt). These chariots were drawn by horses. He also upgraded weaponry by using arrows tipped with iron pieces. He then began fighting the Hyksos, starting from Upper Egypt, where the people rallied around him. He Seqenenre Tao IIed them effectively until they became strong and skilled warriors. He continued battling the Hyksos from Upper Egypt until he reached their capital, which they had established near the modern city of Zagazig. He kept fighting them until they fled to the northern Delta, with him in pursuit—through Sinai and into Palestine. Ahmose did not return until he was certain that Egypt’s eastern borders were secure from them and their attacks, after completely eliminating them. After expelling the Hyksos, Ahmose and his army reached Phoenicia. He also attacked Nubia to reclaim it for the Egyptian kingdom, extending its southern border to the Second Cataract. Ahmose’s campaigns were depicted in the tomb of two of his soldiers: Ahmose son of Ibana and Ahmose son of Nakht. After finishing his wars to expel enemies and secure Egypt’s borders, Ahmose turned his attention to internal affairs, which had collapsed during the Hyksos occupation. He reformed the tax system, reopened trade routes, repaired irrigation canals, and restored the water management system. He also rebuilt destroyed temples and made Thebes his capital, with Amun as the official deity of his era. Ahmose ruled for about a quarter of a century and died around the age of 35. After expelling the Hyksos from Egypt, he returned to the country in 1571 BCE and focused on internal reforms, leading historians to consider him the founder of the New Kingdom.

His Mummy
It is believed that Ahmose had two tombs: 1. One in Abydos, consisting of a sloping temple, a funerary tomb, and the remains of a pyramid discovered in 1899 CE. It was identified as his pyramid in 1902 CE, along with a temple for the pyramid. 2. The other in Thebes, which was looted by thieves. His mummy was discovered in 1881 CE in the Deir el-Bahri cache (DB320) along with mummies of some kings from the Eighteenth, Nineteenth, and Twenty-First Dynasties. It was identified on June 9, 1886, by Gaston Maspero. The mummy measured 1.63 m (5'4") in height and had a relatively small face compared to the size of the chest.

Queen Ahhotep

Ahhotep I (or Iahhotep I) was an ancient Egyptian queen and royal wife who lived at the end of the Seventeenth Dynasty. She was the daughter of Queen Tetisheri and Senakhtenre Ahmose, and likely the sister and wife of King Seqenenre Tao II. Her long life had a notable impact on events in Egypt, especially during the wars of liberation against the Hyksos.

More than one Ahhotep
Over the years, the naming and ordering of the queens called Ahhotep have changed. Below is a summary of these differences: -ate 19th century: It was believed that Ahhotep I was the wife of King Seqenenre II, and some experts thought that both the Deir el-Bahri and Dra’ Abu el-Naga coffins belonged to her. It was also believed that Ahhotep II was the wife of King Amenhotep I, and some thought the Deir el-Bahri cache coffin belonged to Ahhotep II in this context. -ate 20th century: In the 1970s, it was discovered that the Deir el-Bahri coffin bears the title “King’s Mother,” but Amenhotep I had no children, suggesting she might be the mother of Ahmose I. In 1982, American archaeologist Robins proposed that Ahhotep I was the owner of the gilded coffin found in Dra’ Abu el-Naga, Ahhotep II was the queen mentioned on the Deir el-Bahri coffin, and Ahhotep III was the queen mentioned on the statue of Prince Ahmose Sapair. -21st century (present): Following the view of archaeologists Dodson and Hilton (2004), Ahhotep I is the wife of King Seqenenre II and mother of King Ahmose I, while Ahhotep II is the queen known from the gilded coffin found in Dra’ Abu el-Naga and was likely the wife of King Kamose. (There is no Ahhotep III.)

Her Family
Ahhotep I is the daughter of Queen Tetisheri and King Senakhtenre Tao I, and was the wife of King Seqenenre Tao II. It is believed that she was his sister. She is likely the mother of King Ahmose I. Her exact relationship with King Kamose is uncertain, but he may have been her husband’s brother (brother of Seqenenre Tao II) or possibly her son. Among the other children of Queen Ahhotep I are:

  • Queen Ahmose-Nefertari (wife of her brother, King Ahmose I).
  • Prince Ahmose Sapair.
  • Prince Binpu.
  • Princess Ahmose-Henuttamehu.
  • Princess Ahmose-Nebetta.
  • Princess Ahmose-Tumerisi.

Her Life
On a stela from the reign of King Ahmose I at Karnak, it is mentioned that Ahhotep I mobilized the troops and played a role in defending Thebes against the Hyksos. The exact timing of these events is unknown; they may have occurred after the death of Seqenenre or Kamose. Praise was given to the Lady of the Land and Lady of the Aegean Isles, whose name is exalted in every foreign country. She is the one who devises plans for the masses, the King’s Wife and Royal Sister, may she live in prosperity and health. She is the King’s Sister and King’s Mother, the noble and wise one who cares for and manages Egypt’s affairs. She gathered her army, protected the people, brought back the fugitives, reunited the displaced, pacified Upper Egypt (i.e., the Theban kingdom), and subdued its rebels—the Royal Wife, Ahhotep, may she live. Ahhotep is also mentioned on the stela of Kares (Cairo Museum, CG34003), an official who held the position of steward of this queen’s estates. The stela is dated to Year 10 of King Amenhotep I. Her personal servant Iuf mentions her on another stela (CG34009). Iuf refers to Queen Ahhotep on his stela as the mother of Ahmose I. Later, Iuf became a servant of Queen Ahmose, wife of Thutmose I and mother of Hatshepsut, indicating that Ahhotep likely died at a considerably advanced age during the reign of Thutmose I.

Her Titles

  • Great Royal Wife
  • King’s Mother (on the coffin found in the Deir el-Bahri area)

Her Tomb
The coffin of Queen Ahhotep I was re-buried in the Deir el-Bahri cache. The coffin depicts the queen wearing a three-layered wig, and the body is covered with a feathered design. It thus resembles the outer coffins of Queens Ahmose-Nefertari and Ahmose-Merytamun. The original tomb of Queen Ahhotep remains unknown to this day, unless this queen is identical to Ahhotep II. However, the measurements of the coffin found in Dra’ Abu el-Naga show that her body was too large to fit in the Deir el-Bahri coffin. This evidence has been used to argue that Ahhotep I cannot be the same as Queen Ahhotep II.
