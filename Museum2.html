King Thutmose III

Thutmose III officially ruled Egypt from his coronation on April 28, 1479 BCE, when he was two years old, until his death on March 11, 1425 BCE. His military life
Thutmose III focused on the army, making it professional and equipping it with cavalry and war chariots. During his reign, the ancient Egyptians, thanks to him, mastered the craft of making bows and arrows. In contrast to Thutmose‚Äôs era, when Hatshepsut ruled, she followed a peaceful policy with the Egyptian spheres of influence in Palestine and Nubia and with neighboring regions. She focused on the navy and sent maritime expeditions to the land of Punt and to the coasts of Lebanon for trade. Some protectorates in Syria and Mitanni seized the opportunity to rebel against Egyptian rule. As soon as Thutmose III ascended the throne after Hatshepsut‚Äôs death, he had to restore Egyptian control over those regions to secure the country‚Äôs borders. He launched **sixteen military campaigns** in Asia (the regions of Syria and Palestine), through which he consolidated his influence there and extended Egypt‚Äôs control as far south as Nubia. The prince of the city of **Kadesh** in Syria led a coalition of Asian princes in the Levant against Egypt, numbering **twenty-three armies**. It was expected that Thutmose III would reinforce his defenses and forces on the Egyptian border near Sinai. However, Thutmose decided to march with his massive armies to confront these forces on their own soil, as part of a plan to expand the Egyptian Empire to its maximum possible extent and secure the borders against hostile armies.

Battle of Megiddo
Thutmose III built fortresses and strongholds, trained his soldiers with the best drills, and supplied them with powerful innovative weapons such as advanced bows and arrows and expanded the use of chariots in battle. In the **Battle of Megiddo** campaign, he divided his army into a center and two wings and employed military tactics and maneuvers that were previously unknown. He then led his army from Qantara, covering **150 miles in ten days**, reaching Gaza. From there, he marched another **80 miles in eleven days** between Gaza and one of the cities at the foot of Mount Carmel. There, Thutmose III held a war council with his officers after learning that the prince of Kadesh had come to the city of **Megiddo** and gathered around him **230 princes** with their armies. They encamped in the fortified city of Megiddo to halt the advance of Thutmose III and his forces. There were **three routes** to reach Megiddo: two of them circled around the base of Mount Carmel, while the third was a narrow pass but led directly to Megiddo. Thutmose decided that the army would take the third pass‚Äîa daring move that later turned the tide of the battle. The forces of the prince of Kadesh and his allies had positioned themselves at the end of the two wide roads, believing that the Egyptian army would come from one or both of them. At dawn the next day, King Thutmose III ordered the army to reassemble the war chariots and prepare for a surprise attack. His forces charged, with Thutmose at the front leading in a **semicircular formation** toward Megiddo. The surprise was that the Egyptians launched this overwhelming assault, throwing the enemy into confusion and disarray. Their armies fell into chaos and disorder, and the commanders and troops began to flee, abandoning their large chariots and their camp filled with spoils, to take refuge inside the fortified city. Because the Egyptian soldiers became preoccupied with collecting the spoils, the Asians were able to escape into the city and fortify themselves there. The consequence of the army‚Äôs distraction with gathering loot‚Äîwhen they could have completely destroyed the forces of the prince of Kadesh and achieved total victory‚Äîwas that Thutmose III was forced to **besiege Megiddo for seven long months** until the princes surrendered. They sent their sons carrying weapons to surrender them to King Thutmose III.

His architectural works
Thutmose III built numerous temples in Thebes, including two, one of which was adjacent to Hatshepsut‚Äôs temple at Deir el-Bahri. He also constructed the massive **sixth and seventh pylons** and the **Festival Hall** at the Karnak Temple, completed the Temple of Hapou that Hatshepsut had begun, and erected a temple to the god **Ptah** in his homeland at Memphis. This temple contains three chambers: the first for Ptah, the second for **Hathor**, goddess of Thebes, and the third for the goddess **Sekhmet**, wife of Ptah, represented by a statue of her with a lioness head crowned with a sun disk. He also had temples in **Amada** and **Semna**, built a temple on **Elephantine** for the goddess **Satet**, and left remains in **Kom Ombo**, **Edfu**, **Heliopolis**, and **Armant**. Thutmose III erected **at least seven obelisks**, most of which are now in various world capitals: - The obelisk in **London** (one of two he erected in front of the Sun Temple at Heliopolis). A Greek engineer named **Pontius** moved both to Alexandria to place them before the Temple of Isis. This obelisk fell from its base in the 14th century CE. It is said that **Muhammad Ali Pasha** gifted it to Britain in **1831 CE**, but it did not arrive in London until **1878 CE**, having lain on the ground all that time due to transportation difficulties. **Sir Erasmus Wilson** funded its transport and built a special ship for it. The ship nearly sank in a storm in the **Bay of Biscay**, but the obelisk was saved and arrived safely. Notably, this obelisk was damaged by shrapnel during World War II on the **River Thames**. In fact, this obelisk represents only the **upper part** of a much taller original‚Äîlonger than any obelisk existing today. - Another obelisk is in **New York**, erected by Thutmose in front of the Sun Temple. It is the **twin** of the London obelisk and now stands in **Central Park**. - In his later years, Thutmose ordered an obelisk to be placed before the **eighth pylon** at Karnak, but it was left unfinished due to his death. It remained in place for **35 years** until **Thutmose IV** discovered it and erected it in its intended location. It is now in **Rome** in front of the **Basilica of St. John Lateran**. **Constantine the Great**, ruler of the Roman Empire, moved this **455-ton** obelisk to Alexandria in **330 CE** to send it to Byzantium to beautify his new capital, but failed to transport it further. It remained there for **27 years** until his son **Constantius** moved it to Rome and erected it in the **Circus Maximus**. In **1587 CE**, it was rediscovered, broken into **three pieces**. **Domenico Fontana** repaired and restored it on the orders of **Pope Sixtus V** and installed it in its current location in the **Lateran Square**. King Mena

King Menes (Egyptian: Mani; Ancient Greek: ŒúŒÆŒΩŒ∑œÇ; English: Menes) was an ancient Egyptian king from the Early Dynastic Period, one of the kings of Egypt during the Early Dynastic Period in ancient Egypt. He is credited with unifying Upper and Lower Egypt and is considered the founder of the First Dynasty. Menes' identity is a subject of debate among Egyptologists. Some attribute it to King Hor-Aha, while others link it to King Narmer. Both pharaohs are credited with unifying Egypt to varying degrees by different authorities.

His biography
King Menes, Unifier of the Two Lands, was an Egyptian king from the First Egyptian Dynasty in the city of Thebes (modern Luxor). He succeeded in unifying the Two Lands (the northern and southern kingdoms) around 3200 BCE. For this great achievement, he was granted several titles, such as "King of the Two Lands," "Lord of the Two Crowns," "Falcon of the South," and "Cobra of the North." King Menes is considered the founder of the First Dynasty of ancient Egyptian kings.

Origin of the name
The name Menes is mentioned in some ancient Egyptian writings as (Meni), and the Egyptians later altered the name to Mena. Strangely, the word (Meni) means "to establish" or "to build" in the ancient Egyptian language, as if the Egyptians wanted to honor his achievement in his name. Some historians confirm that (Meni) is a verb or title, not a personal name. In the Coptic (Egyptian) language, the name Mena has many synonyms, including (stable, firm, established, enduring, lasting, or founder). Thus, Mena is considered the doer of the verb "Meni" in the Egyptian language. The commonly used name Mena derives from Manetho, an Egyptian historian and priest who lived during the Ptolemaic Kingdom. Manetho recorded the name in Greek as ŒúŒÆŒΩŒ∑œÇ (translated: M√™n√™s). An alternative Greek form, ŒúŒπŒΩ (phonetically translated: Min), was cited by the 5th-century BCE historian Herodotus, but this variant is no longer accepted; it appears to have resulted from contamination with the name of the god Min. The Egyptian form, mnj, is taken from the king lists in Turin and Abydos, dating to the Nineteenth Dynasty, with its pronunciation reconstructed as */maÀànij/. By the early New Kingdom, changes in the Egyptian language led to the name being pronounced */maÀàne î/. The name mnj means "he who endures," which I. E. S. Edwards (1971) suggests may have been coined as "merely a descriptive epithet referring to a semi-legendary hero [...] whose [personal] name has been lost." Rather than a specific individual's name, it may collectively refer to the Naqada III rulers: Ka, Scorpion II, and Narmer.

Creation of the White Wall Fortress
King Menes thought of choosing a site that lay midway between the kingdoms of the North and South so that he could rule Egypt from it. He established a fortress surrounded by a white wall and named it **Men-nefer**, meaning "the beautiful harbor" or "the white wall." Part of it still remains to this day. Memphis became the first capital of Egypt after unification. However, due to the strong influence of the southern city kings, Memphis did not become firmly established as the capital of the country, nor did it reach the height of its power and rule until the Old Kingdom era. Later, the Greeks called it **Memphis** or "the Way of the Rams." When the Arabs conquered Egypt, they named it **Menf**, and it is now the area of **Mit Rahina**, which belongs to the city and center of **Badrshein** in **Giza Governorate**.

Hor-Aha
Hor-Aha is the name of the king who succeeded King Menes. It belongs to the Horus name category among royal titles. The meaning of Hor-Aha is "Horus who fights." It is called the Horus name in reference to the god Horus, and it is written inside the serekh sign‚Äîwhich represents the palace facade and was later used to mean "palace" (as transmitted by the Hebrews to the Arabs as "sarh"). It is also called the Ka name in reference to the Ka. It is one of the oldest names, used by kings of the Predynastic period. He is considered the earthly embodiment of the god Horus, the most important deity in the lives of the ancient Egyptians.

Queen Nefertiti

Queen Nefertiti, whose name means "the beautiful one has come," was the wife of King Amenhotep IV (who later became Akhenaten), the famous pharaoh of the Eighteenth Dynasty, and the mother-in-law of Tutankhamun. She was considered one of the most powerful women in ancient Egypt. She lived for a short time after her husband's death and helped Tutankhamun ascend to the throne. This beautiful queen held a high status during her husband's reign. She belonged to the Eighteenth Dynasty and lived in the 14th century BCE. Like what happened with her husband, her name was erased from historical records and her images were defaced after her death. Nefertiti became famous for the painted limestone bust of her face, one of the finest works of art from the ancient era and the most famous depiction of Queen Nefertiti.

Her life Queen Nefertiti shared with her husband the worship of the new religion, which was the cult of Aten, the power of the sun disk. She and her husband were the intermediaries between the people and Aten. It was assumed that full blessings were granted only when the royal couple united. During the early years of her husband's reign, Nefertiti changed her name in accordance with her new faith to Neferneferuaten Nefertiti, meaning "Aten shines because the beautiful one has come." Nefertiti supported her husband during the religious and social reforms, then moved with him to Akhetaten (Tell el-Amarna). She appeared with him during celebrations and rituals, in family scenes, and even in traditional depictions of military campaigns, where she was shown defeating enemies.

Her bust "bust = half-statue (of the face, neck, and shoulders)" Nefertiti is remembered for the painted and sculpted limestone bust of her face, one of the finest works of art from the ancient era and the most famous depiction of Queen Nefertiti. It was discovered by the German Egyptologist **Ludwig Borchardt** on December 6, 1912, in the workshop of the sculptor Thutmose at Tell el-Amarna. Borchardt smuggled the complete (undamaged) bust to his home in the Zamalek district of Cairo, and from there, he shipped it to Germany, hidden among worthless broken pottery pieces sent to Berlin for restoration. Another bust of Nefertiti‚Äôs head, made of red quartzite and decorated with ink touches, exists in the Egyptian Museum. It is no less precise in craftsmanship than the one in Berlin but is less famous. It is still unknown whether a mummy of Queen Nefertiti exists. What is certain is that scientists found mummies in a secret chamber in Akhenaten‚Äôs tomb. In the 19th century, French archaeologist **Victor Loret**, while opening one of the crypt walls, discovered a side chamber containing three mummies: one male and two female, one younger than the other. At the time, these mummies attracted little attention. They were photographed in 1907 and then forgotten. Recently, however, doubts have arisen among scholars that one of these two female mummies might be Nefertiti. In 2002, British researcher **Joann Fletcher** from the University of York, an expert in mummies, announced that the remains of the younger mummy belonged to Queen Nefertiti, stating it was an indisputable fact. Joyfully, she called it "the greatest discovery of my life!" after being allowed to subject the mummy to X-ray examinations. The strongest evidence that one of these mummies is "the beautiful one who has come" lies in the high quality of the embalming and the anatomical resemblance to known descriptions of Nefertiti‚Äôs remains‚Äîat least as Fletcher saw it in the neck, shoulders, and especially the face. The mummy was bald, shaved to wear a special wig, and Fletcher appears to have found the wig. However, this claim later became highly controversial and disputed by Egyptian authorities. Examinations also revealed traces of a leather headband imprinted on the mummy‚Äôs forehead and evidence of two earrings in the left ear‚Äîfeatures previously seen in some images of the queen. Shortly afterward, a broken hand was found near the mummy, holding a scepter‚Äîa known symbol of pharaonic authority. In contrast, her colleagues expressed skepticism, believing the remains were merely those of a young woman from the royal family who died during the 18th Dynasty. End of her life One of their daughters, Meketaten, died, and their grief over her was depicted in some wall paintings. After their daughter's death, Nefertiti disappeared from the royal court, and her daughter Meritaten took her place, receiving the title of Great Royal Wife. After the twelfth year of Akhenaten's reign, Nefertiti vanished, and no further mention of her exists. It is believed that she died and was buried in a tomb at Akhetaten. It is also thought that Tutankhamun later moved her mummy along with his father Akhenaten's when Akhetaten was abandoned.

Her tomb
Egyptologists have been unable to determine the location of Nefertiti's tomb for years of research following the discovery of Tutankhamun's tomb. In 2015, Dr. Nicholas Reeves from the University of Arizona announced that he may have located the aforementioned queen's tomb and that she might have been secretly buried inside Tutankhamun's tomb. This is because scans revealed the possible existence of an entrance in Tutankhamun's tomb. This door might lead to Queen Nefertiti's burial place. Digital radar scanning showed traces of two locations that were once used as doors. Consequently, Reeves hypothesized the existence of a secret passage leading to other burial chambers. He stated that this is likely the reason why Tutankhamun's tomb is smaller than the other pharaohs' tombs in Egypt.
